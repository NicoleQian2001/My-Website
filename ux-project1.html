The problem

People arrive at the movie theatre late but still want snacks without going through waiting in line to order or people want theatre’s food to be ordered and delivered elsewhere.

The goal

Design an app for the movie theatre that allows users to easily order snacks and schedule a pickup or delivery without accessibility issues.

User research: summary

I conducted interviews and created empathy maps to understand the users I’m designing for and their needs. A primary user group identified through research was busy working adults who arrive at the movie theatre late. This user group confirmed initial assumptions about the movie theatre’s customers, but research also revealed that waiting time to order and pick up snacks was not the only factor preventing users to order snacks from the theatre in person. Other user problems included accessibility issues (visual impairment, language barrier, etc).

Time

Working adults are too busy to arrive at the movie theatre on time to have enough time for ordering snacks before the movie starts.

Accessibility

Many platforms for ordering movie snacks are not equipped with assistive technologies.

IA

Text-heavy menus in apps are often difficult to read and order from.

Unmoderated Usability Testing

I conducted unmoderated usability testing with 7 participants of various ages and residences. The goal is to investigate what specific difficulties users encounter when they try to complete the core tasks of the movie theatre’s snack-ordering app: item selection, ordering, and in-app navigation. I chose the unmoderated testing method since it is less prone to bias, and the observer effect--individuals' acting differently when they are aware that they are being watched--does not apply. Here are a research plan containing a list of prompts.

Here is a selected note-taking spreadsheet from one of the 7 participants. It is evident in the observations and quotes that the participant can't tell the difference between "current location" and "enter location". In fact, this is a common pain point among all participants. This reveals that the initial design of scheduling a delivery page is not intuitive.

Mockups

The early design contained added filters, but after the usability studies, I considered the filters extra and removed them. I added a navigation bar on the bottom to enhance the mobile app experience.

The second usability study reveals that past orders are important and it needs an easier way to navigate to them. I moved it from the navigation dropdown to the navigation bar below the screen.

I added navigation bar on the bottom of the “Home” and “My Orders page” to make the app more like a mobile app. I created “My Orders page” to reorder past orders and/or cancel a history order and a recommendation page based on past orders.

The final high-fidelity prototype presented more complete user flows including cancellation message for cancelled orders.

Accessibility considerations

Provided users who are visually impaired with screen reader.

App available in all languages for users speaking different languages.

Used detailed images containing comparison objects for exhibiting different snack sizes to help all users better understand size difference.

Takeaways

Impact: The app makes users feel like the movie theatre really thinks about how to meet their needs. One quote from peer feedback: “The app made it so easy and fast to order snacks and schedule a delivery! I would definitely use this app instead of ordering snacks in-person. I would also like to order a delivery to home when I crave movie snacks.”

What I learned: While designing the movie theatre’s app, I learned that the first ideas for the app are only the beginning of the process. Usability studies and peer feedback influenced each iteration of the app’s designs.
